About the BBC Micro

The BBC Micro was a series of microcomputers designed and built by Acorn Computers for the BBC Computer Literacy Project. First released in December 1981, it became one of the most successful British computers of the 1980s, particularly dominating the UK education market.

📜 The History of the BBC Micro

The story of the BBC Micro is one of British innovation, fierce competition, and lasting educational legacy.

The Computer Literacy Project (1980-1981)

In 1980, the BBC recognized that Britain was falling behind in the computer revolution. They conceived the Computer Literacy Project - an ambitious plan to educate the British public about computing through television programs and a standardized computer.

The BBC put out a specification for a computer that would accompany their television series. The requirements were demanding:

  • Good graphics and sound capabilities
  • Expandability for education
  • Professional keyboard
  • Multiple I/O ports
  • Ability to run BBC BASIC
  • Reliability for classroom use

Acorn Computers Wins the Contract

Several companies competed for the BBC contract, including Sinclair Research (makers of the ZX80/ZX81). However, Acorn Computers, a small Cambridge-based company founded by Chris Curry and Hermann Hauser, won the contract with their prototype based on the 6502 processor.

The story goes that Acorn had just five days to build a working prototype for the BBC's evaluation. Working around the clock, engineer Sophie Wilson (then Roger Wilson) and Steve Furber created a machine that not only met the BBC's requirements but exceeded them.

The Acorn vs Sinclair Rivalry

🎬 The Battle for British Computing

The rivalry between Acorn and Sinclair Research became one of the most famous business battles in British technology history. Clive Sinclair and Chris Curry were former colleagues who had worked together at Sinclair Radionics before Curry left to found Acorn.

Sinclair's Approach: Clive Sinclair focused on making computers affordable for the mass market. His ZX Spectrum (1982, £125-£175) was cheap, colorful, and sold millions but was seen as more of a games machine.

Acorn's Approach: The BBC Micro (£235-£399) was more expensive but professional, expandable, and backed by the BBC's educational mission. It dominated schools while Sinclair dominated homes.

Launch and Success (1981-1984)

The BBC Micro launched in December 1981 alongside the BBC's television series "The Computer Programme." Initial demand was so high that Acorn struggled to keep up with orders, with waiting lists extending months.

Key Success Factors:

  • BBC Endorsement: The "BBC" branding gave it credibility and trust
  • Education Focus: Government funding helped schools buy them
  • Superior BASIC: BBC BASIC was one of the best BASIC dialects ever created
  • Expandability: The Tube interface and ROM sockets allowed serious expansion
  • Build Quality: Robust construction suitable for classroom abuse
  • Software Support: Educational publishers created thousands of programs

The Golden Age (1982-1987)

By 1983, the BBC Micro dominated British education. Schools across the UK standardized on it, creating an entire generation familiar with BBC BASIC and 6502 assembly language.

Notable Achievements:

  • Elite (1984): Groundbreaking 3D space game created on BBC Micro
  • Second Processors: ARM processor development began as BBC co-processor
  • Domesday Project (1986): Digital archive of Britain using BBC Micros and LaserDiscs
  • Econet Networks: Schools networked BBC Micros years before PCs

BBC Master and Evolution (1986-1988)

In 1986, Acorn released the BBC Master 128, an enhanced BBC Micro with 128KB RAM and improved capabilities. While successful, it competed against increasingly powerful PCs and couldn't match the Model B's dominance.

Variants included the Master Turbo (with 65C102 co-processor), Master Compact (all-in-one design), and Master ET (Econet Terminal).

Decline and Legacy (1989-Present)

By the late 1980s, IBM PC compatibles and new 16-bit home computers eroded the BBC Micro's market position. Acorn shifted focus to the Archimedes (based on ARM) and eventually became ARM Holdings, whose processors now power billions of smartphones.

The BBC Micro's Lasting Impact:

  • ARM Architecture: Developed from BBC Micro co-processor, now dominates mobile computing
  • Programming Education: Inspired a generation of UK programmers and game developers
  • Elite Legacy: Led to Elite: Dangerous and influenced space game genre
  • Raspberry Pi: Spiritual successor, created by Acorn alumni to inspire new generation
  • BBC micro:bit: Modern educational computer keeping BBC's computing mission alive
🎓 Educational Legacy

Many of Britain's leading programmers, game developers, and computer scientists cite the BBC Micro as their introduction to computing. Companies like Rockstar Games (Grand Theft Auto), ARM Holdings, and numerous game studios were founded or staffed by people who learned on BBC Micros.

The BBC Micro proved that educational computing could be both rigorous and accessible - a lesson that continues to influence modern initiatives like the Raspberry Pi Foundation.

Educational Revolution

The BBC Micro achieved something unprecedented: it put a powerful, consistent computer in nearly every British school. This created a generation of programmers, engineers, and tech entrepreneurs who learned on the same platform.

In Schools

80% of UK schools had BBC Micros by 1983. Students learned BBC BASIC, turtle graphics (Logo), and problem-solving. Many future tech leaders credit the BBC Micro for their start.

At Home

While more expensive than Spectrum, BBC Micro attracted serious programmers and families valuing education. The "family computer" for homework and learning.

In Industry

Used for control systems, data logging, embedded applications. The expandability and professional build quality made it suitable for serious work.

The ARM Legacy

💡 From BBC Micro to Your Smartphone

Acorn's work on the BBC Micro led directly to the ARM processor. When Acorn needed a faster processor for the Archimedes (BBC Micro successor), they designed their own: the ARM (Acorn RISC Machine).

Today, ARM processors (now Arm Holdings) power:

  • 99% of smartphones worldwide (iPhone, Android)
  • Most tablets and embedded devices
  • Apple Silicon (M1, M2, M3, M4 chips)
  • Raspberry Pi computers
  • Over 250 billion ARM chips shipped

The BBC Micro's educational mission inadvertently created the most successful processor architecture in history!

The End of an Era

By the late 1980s, the market had shifted. IBM PC compatibility became essential for business, and games consoles dominated home entertainment. The BBC Micro's educational focus became a limitation.

What Happened to Acorn?

1987 - Archimedes

ARM-based successor. Powerful but expensive. Limited commercial success outside education.

1990 - ARM Spun Off

ARM Holdings created as joint venture with Apple. The real legacy begins.

1998 - Acorn Dissolved

Company broken up. Element 14 became Broadcom, RISC OS continued independently.

What Happened to Sinclair?

Sinclair's later computers (QL, C5 electric vehicle) were commercial failures. Amstrad bought the computer business in 1986. Sinclair himself continued inventing but never regained his 1980s success. Sir Clive Sinclair passed away in 2021, remembered as a British computing pioneer.

📊 Final Score

Commercial Success: Sinclair Spectrum won (5M vs 1.5M units)

Educational Impact: BBC Micro won (defined UK computer education)

Long-term Legacy: BBC Micro won (ARM processors in 250B+ devices)

Both companies achieved remarkable things with limited resources. British computing wouldn't be the same without either of them.

📊 Complete Screen MODE Comparison Guide

Understanding Screen Modes

The BBC Micro offers 8 screen modes (0-7) with different resolutions, colors, and memory usage. Choosing the right MODE is fundamental to programming efficiency.

Key Trade-off: Higher resolution = less colors. More colors = lower resolution. Lower memory = more space for program.

📝 Practical MODE Selection Guide

🎮 For Games:
  • MODE 7: Classic choice - 31KB for code! Elite, Chuckie Egg, Defender. Distinctive Teletext look.
  • MODE 4: Good compromise - 22KB free, decent resolution. Repton, Citadel.
  • MODE 5: 22KB free with 4 colors. Many arcade games.
  • MODE 1: Better graphics, 12KB. Graphically impressive games.
📊 For Productivity:
  • MODE 0: Word processing - 80 column essential for serious work.
  • MODE 3: 80×25 text for programming, editors.
  • MODE 6: 40 column when you need more memory.
🎨 For Graphics & Art:
  • MODE 2: Full 16 colors - paint programs, colorful menus.
  • MODE 1: Best balance - resolution + color for demos.
  • MODE 0: Hi-res CAD, technical diagrams.
💡 MODE 7 Special Features

MODE 7 uses the SAA5050 Teletext chip - separate hardware with built-in character set and semi-graphics blocks. Elite used MODE 7 with custom character definitions for its wireframe 3D space + dashboard!

🎵 SOUND Command - Complete Syntax

SOUND channel, amplitude, pitch, duration
Parameter Range Description
channel 0-3 or &10-&13 Sound channel: 0-2 = tone, 3 = noise. Add &10 to flush sound queue and sync.

⚡ BASIC Optimization Tips

Make Your Programs Faster and Smaller!

BBC BASIC is fast, but these techniques can make it even faster. Learn how to optimize for speed and memory!

⚡ Speed Optimization Techniques

1. Use Integer Variables (30-50% faster)

REM SLOW - floating point
10 FOR I=1 TO 1000: NEXT

REM FAST - integer variables (note % suffix)
20 FOR I%=1 TO 1000: NEXT

2. Minimize GOTO/GOSUB Depth

REM SLOW - deep nesting
10 GOSUB 1000
1000 GOSUB 2000
2000 GOSUB 3000

REM FASTER - flat structure
10 PROC_init
20 PROC_main
30 PROC_cleanup

3. Use PROCs and FNs Wisely

REM PROCs slightly slower but much more readable
DEF PROC_draw_sprite(X%,Y%,C%)
  GCOL 0,C%
  MOVE X%,Y%: PLOT 153,X%+16,Y%+16
ENDPROC

REM Use for repeated complex operations
FOR I%=0 TO 10
  PROC_draw_sprite(I%*50,100,I% MOD 4)
NEXT

4. Optimize Loops

REM Count down instead of up (slightly faster)
FOR I%=1000 TO 0 STEP -1: NEXT

REM Avoid calculations inside loops
REM SLOW:
FOR I%=0 TO 100
  X%=I%*640/100  : REM Calculated 101 times!
NEXT

REM FAST:
STEP%=640/100
FOR I%=0 TO 100
  X%=I%*STEP%
NEXT

💾 Memory Optimization

1. Variable Packing

REM Use single-letter variables (faster lookup)
REM SLOW: playerX, playerY, playerHealth
REM FAST: X%, Y%, H%

REM Pack booleans into single byte
flags%=0
flagVisible%=1      : REM Bit 0
flagActive%=2       : REM Bit 1
flagInvincible%=4   : REM Bit 2

REM Check: IF flags% AND flagVisible% THEN ...

2. String Handling

REM Pre-allocate strings to avoid garbage collection
DIM A$(100)  : REM 100 bytes allocated
A$="Hello"   : REM Uses pre-allocated space

REM Avoid repeated concatenation
REM SLOW:
S$=""
FOR I%=1 TO 100
  S$=S$+STR$(I%)  : REM Creates 100 temporary strings!
NEXT

REM FASTER: Build in array, join once
DIM S$(100)
FOR I%=1 TO 100: S$(I%)=STR$(I%): NEXT

Control Lines CB1 (input), CB2 (input/output)
Voltage TTL levels: 0V = low, +5V = high
Current ~1mA per pin (use buffers for higher current)
Power Available +5V @ 100mA max (pins 13, 14)
Access Method OSBYTE &97-&9F or direct VIA access at &FE60

🔊 Startup Beep Code Guide

Diagnostic Beep Patterns on Power-Up

The BBC Micro performs a Power-On Self-Test (POST) and uses beep codes to indicate hardware status. Listen carefully to the number and pattern of beeps immediately after switching on.

Beeps Meaning Likely Cause Action Required
1 Beep ✅ Normal Startup All self-tests passed None - system ready!
2 Beeps Keyboard Not Found Keyboard connector (PL10) disconnected or Serial ULA (IC7) failure Check keyboard cable connection, reseat connector
3 Beeps ROM Checksum Error OS ROM (IC20/IC21) failure or corruption Reseat ROM chips, test with known good ROMs
4 Beeps RAM Failure (First 8K) RAM chips IC53-60 failure, critical for OS operation Test/replace RAM using binary search method
5 Beeps RAM Failure (Second 8K) RAM chips IC61-68 failure Test/replace RAM chips in second bank
6 Beeps RAM Failure (Third 8K) Additional RAM bank failure (if installed) Test/replace RAM in third bank
7 Beeps RAM Failure (Fourth 8K) Fourth RAM bank failure (32K models) Test/replace RAM in fourth bank
Continuous Beeping Critical Hardware Failure CPU not running, no clock, PSU failure, or ULA failure Check PSU voltages, CPU clock signal, reseat ICs
No Beep No Power or Complete Failure PSU dead, CPU not running, no power to speaker Test PSU first, check power LED, verify mains input
💡 Pro Tips for Beep Code Diagnosis:
  • Count Carefully: The beeps happen quickly - count from 1 when you power on
  • Multiple Attempts: Power cycle 2-3 times to confirm the beep count is consistent
  • Speaker Connection: Ensure internal speaker is connected - some beep codes are silent if speaker is disconnected
  • External Speaker: If internal speaker is faulty, connect external speaker to audio out to hear beeps
  • Pattern Recognition: 4-7 beeps always indicate RAM issues in specific 8K banks
  • First Action: For any error beeps, start by reseating all socketed ICs

RAM Bank Mapping (for Beep Codes 4-7)

Understanding which RAM chips correspond to each beep code:

Beep Code RAM Bank IC Numbers Memory Address
4 Beeps First 8K (Critical) IC53-IC60 $0000-$1FFF
5 Beeps Second 8K IC61-IC68 $2000-$3FFF
6 Beeps Third 8K (if fitted) Additional RAM ICs $4000-$5FFF
7 Beeps Fourth 8K (if fitted) Additional RAM ICs $6000-$7FFF
💡 Critical RAM Bank: The first 8K (beep code 4) contains zero page ($0000-$00FF), stack ($0100-$01FF), and OS workspace - this MUST work or the machine won't boot!
✅ Quick Diagnostic Workflow Using Beep Codes:
  1. Power On: Listen carefully and count beeps
  2. 1 Beep: Success! Machine is working - proceed to use
  3. 2 Beeps: Check keyboard connector PL10, ensure firmly seated
  4. 3 Beeps: Reseat ROM chips IC20 and IC21, verify orientation (notch toward center)
  5. 4-7 Beeps: RAM fault - use binary search method to isolate bad chip in indicated bank
  6. Continuous: Critical failure - start with PSU voltage tests
  7. No Beep: Dead PSU most likely - test power rails before proceeding

🔍 Systematic Fault Diagnosis Process

Step 1: Initial Assessment

  1. Document the Symptoms: Write down exactly what happens (or doesn't happen)
  2. Visual Inspection: Look for obvious damage, burnt components, bulging capacitors
  3. Smell Test: Burnt smell indicates failed component (capacitor, IC, resistor)
  4. Check Connections: Ensure all cables, keyboard connector properly seated

Step 2: Power Supply Verification

⚡ ALWAYS test PSU first - most common failure point!

Power Supply Voltage Tests:

Rail Expected Voltage Tolerance Test Point
+5V 5.0V ±0.25V (4.75-5.25V) PSU Pin 3 (Orange)
-5V -5.0V ±0.5V (-4.5 to -5.5V) PSU Pin 2 (Blue)
0V (GND) 0V Reference PSU Pin 1 (Black)

Use dummy load (10Ω 5W resistor on +5V) or test jig. Faulty PSU can damage motherboard components.

🔧 Common Fault Patterns & Solutions

❌ Dead - No Power LED

Likely Causes:

  • PSU failure (90%)
  • Blown fuse in PSU
  • Mains input fault
  • Power switch failure

Test Procedure:

  1. Check mains fuse (F1 in PSU)
  2. Test PSU outputs with DMM
  3. Check for bulging/leaking capacitors
  4. Replace C1, C2 (Rifa capacitors - common failure)
⚠️ Power LED On - No Video

Likely Causes:

  • Video ULA failure (IC6)
  • 6845 CRTC failure (IC2)
  • RAM failure preventing boot
  • CPU not running (IC1)

Test Procedure:

  1. Check +5V at IC1 pin 8 (should be 5.0V)
  2. Test clock signal at IC1 pin 39 (2MHz)
  3. Check video sync output (oscilloscope helpful)
  4. Reseat all socketed ICs
  5. Test RAM with diagnostic ROM if available
🎨 Corrupted/Garbage Video

Likely Causes:

  • Faulty RAM (most common)
  • Poor IC socket contact
  • Address/data bus fault
  • ROM failure (IC20/IC21)

Test Procedure:

  1. Reseat all RAM chips (IC53-68)
  2. Remove half the RAM - test which bank fails
  3. Replace suspect RAM chips one by one
  4. Check ROM checksums if possible
⌨️ Keyboard Not Working

Likely Causes:

  • Keyboard connector loose (check PL10)
  • Serial ULA failure (IC7)
  • Keyboard membrane worn out
  • System VIA failure (IC3)

Test Procedure:

  1. Reseat keyboard connector firmly
  2. Check connector pins not bent
  3. Test IC3 (6522 VIA) - check datasheet
  4. Replace keyboard membrane if worn

💾 RAM Testing & Diagnosis

RAM Fault Symptoms:

  • Random crashes: Bad RAM chip or 120ns RAM (need 100ns)
  • Wrong RAM size reported: One or more RAM chips failed
  • Garbage characters: Data bus corruption, bad RAM
  • Programs load but crash: Intermittent RAM failure

Binary Search Method for RAM:

  1. Remove half the RAM (e.g., IC53-60 or IC61-68)
  2. Power on - does fault change?
  3. If fault persists, bad chip is in remaining bank
  4. Subdivide again - test 4 chips at a time
  5. Continue until faulty chip isolated

🔌 Power Supply Repair Guide

⚡ Common PSU Failures (Priority Order):

1. Rifa Capacitors (C1, C2) - 80% of PSU failures

Symptoms: Smoke, burning smell, won't power on, blown mains fuse

Location: Near mains input, blue cylindrical capacitors

Replacement: X2 class 100nF 275VAC capacitors (£1-2 each)

Why they fail: Polyester film degrades after 40 years, absorbs moisture

⚠️ Replace BOTH even if only one failed!

2. Large Filter Capacitors (C9, C11) - 15% of failures

Symptoms: Unstable voltage, ripple on power rails, crashes under load

Location: Large cylinders near heatsink, typically 4700μF or 6800μF

Testing: ESR meter shows high resistance (>0.5Ω bad), bulging top, leaking

Replacement: Same value, 16V or higher, 105°C rated (£2-5 each)

3. Voltage Regulator Failures - 5% of failures

Symptoms: Wrong voltage on 5V rail, overheating heatsink

Common chips: 7805 (5V regulator), 7905 (-5V regulator)

Testing: Check input/output voltages, thermal shutdown indicates failure

Replacement: Exact replacement or modern equivalent (£1-3)

🔨 Advanced Repair Techniques

Socket Replacement for Unreliable ICs

When to replace sockets:

  • ICs work when pressed but fail when released
  • Corrosion visible in socket
  • Contact pins bent or damaged
  • Intermittent faults that improve with reseating

Procedure:

  1. Use desoldering pump or wick on all pins
  2. Gently rock socket to break free from pads
  3. Clean pads with isopropyl alcohol
  4. Install new machined-pin IC socket
  5. Use flux and fresh solder for clean joints
💡 Pro Tip: For critical ICs (CPU, ULAs, CRTC), use turned-pin or machined-pin sockets for better contact than standard leaf-spring sockets.

Trace Repair for Damaged PCBs

Common PCB damage:

  • Lifted pads from aggressive desoldering
  • Broken traces from physical damage
  • Corrosion from battery leaks or liquid damage

Repair method:

  1. Identify both ends of broken trace with multimeter continuity test
  2. Scrape away solder mask carefully to expose copper
  3. Use 30AWG Kynar wire for jumper repair
  4. Solder wire to exposed copper, secure with epoxy
  5. Test continuity after repair

📋 Step-by-Step Repair Guides

🔧 Complete PSU Rebuild - Step by Step

Video Guides: "BBC Micro PSU Recap" by Adrian's Digital Basement | Nigel's Retro Workshop PSU Repair series

Written Guide: BeebMaster PSU Capacitor Replacement (http://beebmaster.co.uk)

Steps:
  1. Safety: Unplug mains, wait 15 minutes for cap discharge
  2. Document: Photograph PSU before disassembly
  3. Discharge: Short +5V to GND with 10Ω resistor to be safe
  4. Rifa First: Remove C1, C2 (blue caps near mains) - these fail explosively
  5. Filter Caps: Remove C9, C11 (large cylinders near heatsink)
  6. Small Caps: Replace all electrolytics (C3-C8, etc.)
  7. Check Regulators: Test 7805, 7905 (replace if hot or wrong voltage)
  8. Clean Board: Isopropyl alcohol, soft brush
  9. Install New: Match capacitor values and voltage ratings
  10. Test: Use dummy load (10Ω 5W on +5V) before connecting to computer
  11. Voltage Check: +5V = 5.0V ±0.25V, -5V = -5.0V ±0.5V
  12. Final: Reconnect to motherboard, power on, test

Parts List: C1,C2: X2 100nF 275VAC | C9,C11: 4700μF 16V 105°C | Others: Per schematic

Cost: £10-15 for complete recap kit

Time: 1-2 hours for first-time, 30 mins when experienced

💾 RAM Testing and Replacement - Step by Step

Video Guides: "BBC Micro RAM Repair" by RMC - The Cave | Nigel's Retro Workshop testing/diagnosis videos

Forum Thread: StarDot "RAM Fault Finding" sticky

Steps:
  1. Symptom Check: Garbage on screen? Wrong RAM size? Random crashes?
  2. Power Off: Disconnect mains completely
  3. Ground Yourself: Anti-static wrist strap essential
  4. Binary Search Start: Remove RAM IC53-60 (left bank)
  5. Power On Test: Does fault change? Note results
  6. Narrow Down: Remove half of remaining bank
  7. Isolate Chip: Continue until single faulty chip found
  8. Double Check: Swap suspect chip with known good - fault moves?
  9. Replace: Install new 4164 (100ns or faster)
  10. Socket Check: Clean socket with DeoxIT contact cleaner
  11. Test: Run BASIC RAM test program (see above)
  12. Full Test: Load software, play games for 30 mins

Parts: 4164-10 DRAM (100ns) - Buy pack of 20 for £20-30

Alternative: Can use 4164-12 (120ns) but may cause timing issues in some machines

Time: 15-45 minutes depending on which chip is faulty

🎮 Cassette Loading Tips & Tricks

The Authentic BBC Micro Experience!

Cassette tapes were the primary storage medium for BBC Micros in the 1980s. Loading from tape requires patience and the right technique, but it's deeply nostalgic. Here's everything you need for successful tape loading!

📻 Volume Level Settings - Critical!

Getting volume right is 80% of successful loading:

  • Too Quiet: BBC can't detect data → "Load error" or silence
  • Too Loud: Signal clips/distorts → "Bad program" or corrupted data
  • Sweet Spot: About 60-70% volume on tape player
  • Visual Check: Power LED should flicker rapidly during loading (shows data being read)
  • Audio Check: You should hear modulated tones - not silence, not distortion

⚙️ *TAPE Speed Adjustment

REM Set tape speed (1200 baud default)
*TAPE
*TAPE 1200

REM For faster loading (300 baud - more reliable)
*TAPE 300

REM Check current setting
*TAPE

Note: Most commercial software uses 1200 baud. Try 300 baud if you have loading problems.

🎵 Loading from Tape - Step by Step

Basic Loading Procedure:

  1. Insert cassette in tape recorder
  2. Rewind to start (or find program counter position)
  3. Connect tape recorder to BBC cassette port
  4. Set volume to 60-70%
  5. Type: CHAIN "" (for BASIC programs) or *RUN (for machine code)
  6. Press RETURN on BBC
  7. Press PLAY on tape recorder immediately
  8. Wait... (typically 1-5 minutes depending on program size)
  9. Program should auto-run when loaded

REM Save BASIC program
SAVE "MYGAME"
(Press RECORD + PLAY on tape recorder when prompted)

REM Save machine code
*SAVE CODE 1900 2000

REM Save with filename shown on tape counter
SAVE "GAME1"

🌐 Econet & Networking Guide

BBC Micro Networking - Ahead of Its Time!

Econet was Acorn's local area network system, allowing up to 254 BBC Micros to share files, printers, and communicate. Revolutionary in the 1980s, Econet made the BBC Micro the educational networking pioneer!

📡 What is Econet?

Econet Basics:

  • Released: 1981 - one of the earliest microcomputer networks!
  • Speed: 200-400 Kbps (fast for the era)
  • Topology: Bus or star topology using coaxial or twisted-pair cable
  • Stations: Up to 254 devices on a single network (station numbers 1-254)
  • Purpose: File sharing, printer sharing, messaging between computers
  • Usage: Dominated UK schools in 1980s - every classroom networked!

🔧 Hardware Requirements

Component Description
Econet Module Plugs into BBC Micro Econet socket. Contains 68B54 ADLC chip + clock.
Econet Cable 5-pin DIN connectors, coaxial or twisted-pair. Daisy-chain between stations.
Terminator Plugs Required at both ends of network to prevent signal reflection.
Clock Box Provides network-wide clock signal. One per network.
File Server BBC Micro with hard drive (SCSI/IDE) running file server software.

💾 Econet File Server Levels

Acorn developed progressive server software:

  • Level 1 Server: Basic file sharing, single disc drive, limited users
  • Level 2 Server: Multiple users, user directories, better security
  • Level 3 Server: Hard drive support, many users, printer queuing
  • Level 4 Server: Advanced - Master 128 with large hard drives, comprehensive user management
  • SJ MDFS: Third-party enhanced server with modern storage support

🔧 Quick Troubleshooting Guide

Common Problems & Solutions

No Power / Black Screen

Symptoms: Nothing happens when powered on, no lights, no sound
  • Check power cable and fuse (5A in plug)
  • Check PSU fuse on board (usually 1.5A)
  • Test PSU voltages: +5V, -5V, +12V (recap if drifting)
  • Listen for relay click on power-on

Garbled Screen / No Picture

Symptoms: Screen shows random characters, lines, or nothing
  • Try different MODE (press CTRL+0 to 7)
  • Check video cable connections
  • ULA chip failure (IC20 or IC17) - needs GODIL replacement
  • RAM failure - test with diagnostics ROM
  • Try RGB output instead of composite/RF

Keyboard Not Working

Symptoms: Keys don't respond or wrong characters
  • Test with "TYPE 'A'" command (quotes important)
  • Check keyboard ribbon cable connection
  • Keyboard membrane worn out (£15-30 replacement)
  • Clean contacts with isopropyl alcohol
  • IC5 (74LS251) or IC3 (74LS163) failure possible

Won't Boot / Stuck at Startup

Symptoms: "BBC Computer" message but hangs
  • Try BREAK key (DELETE on some keyboards)
  • Remove all ROM chips except OS, test one by one
  • Corrupted ROM chip - replace or reprogram
  • RAM failure - run RAM test (*FX5)
  • Check for stuck keys

Disk Drive Issues

Symptoms: "Bad drive", "Drive fault", won't load
  • Check drive cable (40-pin ribbon)
  • Clean drive heads with isopropyl alcohol
  • Test with known good disk
  • Drive belt may be stretched/broken
  • 8271 FDC chip failure (consider GoSDC upgrade)
  • Try *DRIVE 0 or *DRIVE 2 to select drive

No Sound

Symptoms: No audio from speaker or audio output
  • Test with: SOUND 1,-15,100,20
  • Check internal speaker connection
  • IC21 (SN76489) sound chip failure
  • Volume control may be turned down
  • Audio amplifier IC may have failed

*FX5          - Show operating system version
*FX0          - Get OS version byte
*FX200,n      - Set keyboard auto-repeat delay
*FX201,n      - Set keyboard auto-repeat rate
*FX2,n        - Select input stream

*FX4,n        - Cursor keys mode

*HELP DFS     - Show DFS version

Emergency Recovery

⚠️ If All Else Fails:
  1. Power cycle: Turn off, wait 30 seconds, power on
  2. Remove all addons: Disconnect everything except power and video
  3. Remove all ROMs: Pull all ROMs except OS (far right socket)
  4. Test in MODE 7: Minimal RAM usage for testing
  5. Check Nigel's Retro Workshop: Download fault-finding guides and watch repair videos
  6. Join StarDot forums: Post symptoms with photos for expert help

Phase 3: Component Preparation (1-2 weeks)

Harvest Components from Donor Boards

Critical Components to Salvage:

  1. Serial ULA (IC7): 28-pin DIP, carefully desolder
  2. Video ULA (IC6): 28-pin DIP (unless using VideoNuLA)
  3. Cassette Relay (RY1): Note orientation before removal
  4. 6845 CRTC (IC2): If not buying new
  5. DIN connectors: All 5-pin and 7-pin sockets

Desoldering Tips:

  • Work slowly - ULAs are irreplaceable!
  • Add fresh solder to old joints first (helps heat transfer)
  • Use desoldering pump or wick on each pin
  • Gently rock IC side-to-side to check if loose
  • Never force - better to damage board than IC
  • Clean pins with isopropyl alcohol after removal
  • Store in anti-static foam
Modify 4164 RAM Chips

Required modification for each chip:

  1. Carefully bend pin 1 outward (or cut with flush cutters)
  2. Solder wire jumper: Pin 9 (A7) to Pin 8 (VCC)
  3. Use 30AWG wire for neat connection
  4. Test resistance: Pin 9 to Pin 8 should be ~0Ω
  5. Visual check: Pin 1 not touching socket when inserted

⚠️ Important: Modify ALL 16 chips before starting assembly. Sort by speed grade (100ns separate from 120ns).

Phase 4: PCB Assembly (2-3 weeks)

⚡ Assembly Order is CRITICAL! Follow this sequence to avoid rework and make troubleshooting easier.

Step 1: Install Passive Components (Week 1)

Install in this order:

  1. All resistors (R1-R90+) - bend, insert, solder, trim
  2. All capacitors (C1-C50+) - check polarity on electrolytics!
  3. Crystal (X1) - 16MHz
  4. All diodes (D1-D5) - note polarity stripe!
  5. Transistor (Q3) - match orientation to silkscreen

Quality Checks:

  • Visual: All solder joints shiny and cone-shaped
  • No solder bridges between adjacent pads
  • Component legs trimmed flush to board
  • Polarity marked components oriented correctly
Step 2: Install IC Sockets (Week 1-2)

Socket installation order:

  1. IC1: 40-pin (CPU) - note pin 1 orientation
  2. IC2: 40-pin (CRTC)
  3. IC3, IC69: 40-pin (VIAs)
  4. IC6, IC7: 28-pin (ULAs) - CRITICAL alignment!
  5. IC53-68: 16-pin (RAM) - all 16 sockets
  6. IC20, IC21: 24-pin or 28-pin (ROM sockets)
  7. All logic ICs: 14-pin and 16-pin sockets

Soldering Tips:

  • Use machined pin IC sockets for critical ICs (CPU, ULAs)
  • Standard sockets OK for logic and RAM
  • Align notch with silkscreen before soldering
  • Solder opposite corner pins first to hold socket
  • Check socket is flush against PCB before continuing
  • Solder all remaining pins
Step 3: Install Connectors & Relay (Week 2)
  1. Power connector (7-way from PSU)
  2. Video connectors (RGB, Composite)
  3. All DIN sockets (Cassette, RS423, etc.)
  4. Keyboard connector
  5. User port connector (if fitted)
  6. RY1 Relay: Match orientation carefully!

⚠️ Relay Installation: The cassette relay must match exact orientation from donor board. Incorrect orientation = no cassette functionality.

⚠️ DO NOT install all ICs at once! Progressive installation allows easier troubleshooting.

Installation Sequence (Test After Each Stage)

Stage 1: Power & Clock ICs

Install: IC16 (555), IC40, IC43, IC44, IC45 (clock logic)

Test: Check +5V on all IC power pins with multimeter

Expected: Stable 5.0V ±0.25V on all VCC pins

Stage 2: CPU & Support

Install: IC1 (CPU), IC3 (System VIA), IC2 (CRTC)

Test: Check clock signals on IC1 pins 37 & 39

Expected: 2MHz clock on PHI2 (pin 39)

Stage 3: Video ULA & Serial ULA

Install: IC6 (Video ULA), IC7 (Serial ULA)

Test: Check for video sync on RGB connector

Expected: Sync pulses visible on oscilloscope

Stage 4: RAM Chips

Install: IC53-68 (all 16 RAM chips)

Check: Pin 1 of modified chips not touching socket

Stage 5: ROM Chips

Install: IC20 (OS ROM), IC21 (BASIC ROM)

Verify: Correct orientation (pin 1 notch!)

Test: System should attempt boot

Stage 6: Remaining Logic & I/O

Install: All remaining 74LS chips, IC18 (sound), IC69 (User VIA)

Final test: Full system boot

Pre-Power Checks

Before connecting power:

  • ✅ Visual inspection: No solder bridges, all joints good

  • ✅ Polarity of all electrolytic capacitors correct
  • ✅ Power supply outputs correct voltages (5V, -5V)
